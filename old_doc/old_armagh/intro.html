Armagh ETL

The Irish saved civilization. Literally.

In the Middle Ages, the job of preserving and disseminating the written word fell to many talented monks. The process they employed was a model of efficiency and a good example for those wishing to follow in their footsteps.

The center of activity was the scriptorium. In each monastic scriptorium across Europe, the armarius ("provisioner") collected piles of documents from various sources and made them available to scribes to copy and illuminate. Scribes painstakingly took to their tasks, transcribing the original works. As collections of works were completed, other religious and secular customers sent their librarians to the scriptorium to collect them.

Some documents were in bad condition and what the provisioner recovered were just scraps of documents. These scraps were passed to archivists who would organize them, patch them together, and pass the patchworks to the scribes, who would in turn transcribe as always.

Each scriptorium was run under the watchful eyes of the Abbot and his assistant, the Prior. They made sure that the scriptorium was adequately staffed for its current workload, and that work was flowing as it should. Above them - and outside the scriptorium proper - was the Bishop, who established and maintained agreements with sources and customers and provided the scriptorium's basic resources.

The most renowned scriptorium in Ireland was at Armagh.
